Module 2.4: Project Management for Instructional Design

Creating Realistic Project Timelines

Importance of Timeline Development

A realistic project timeline is essential for successful instructional design projects. It serves as a roadmap that guides the allocation of resources, coordinates the work of team members, and establishes expectations with stakeholders about when deliverables will be completed. Research shows that projects with detailed timelines are 60% more likely to be completed on schedule compared to projects with high-level or informal scheduling approaches (Project Management Institute, 2021).

Timeline development in instructional design must account for the iterative nature of the design process, including multiple review cycles, stakeholder feedback incorporation, and quality assurance activities. Unlike linear production processes, instructional design requires built-in flexibility to accommodate the creative and collaborative aspects of educational content development (Greer & Lin, 2021).

Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) Development

Before creating a timeline, instructional designers must develop a comprehensive work breakdown structure that identifies all tasks required to complete the project. A robust WBS hierarchically and faithfully reflects all project tasks and work packages so that projects are easier to manage (Applied Sciences, 2020). The WBS serves as the foundation for timeline development by breaking down complex projects into manageable, estimable work packages.

For instructional design projects, the WBS typically follows the phases of the chosen instructional design model (such as ADDIE) while incorporating project management activities such as stakeholder communication, quality assurance, and project monitoring. Each work package should be defined at a level where time and resource requirements can be accurately estimated (Dick et al., 2015).

A well-structured WBS for online course development might include work packages for needs analysis, learner analysis, content analysis, learning objective development, instructional strategy design, content development, media production, assessment development, course assembly, testing, and implementation planning (Morrison et al., 2019).

Task Sequencing and Dependencies

Once the WBS is complete, the next step is to identify the logical sequence of tasks and their dependencies. Some tasks must be completed before others can begin (finish-to-start dependencies), while others can be performed simultaneously to optimize the project timeline. Understanding these relationships is crucial for creating realistic timelines and identifying the critical pathâ€”the sequence of tasks that determines the minimum project duration (Kerzner, 2017).

In instructional design projects, common dependencies include the need to complete learner analysis before designing instructional strategies, the requirement to finalize content before beginning media production, and the necessity of completing individual modules before course integration and testing can begin (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001).

Effective dependency management also requires consideration of resource constraints, particularly when working with subject matter experts, graphic designers, or other specialists who may have limited availability or competing priorities (Allen & Gardner, 2014).

Time Estimation Techniques

Accurate time estimation is one of the most challenging aspects of project timeline development. Instructional designers can use several techniques to improve estimation accuracy, including analogous estimating (using historical data from similar projects), parametric estimating (using statistical relationships between variables), and three-point estimating (considering optimistic, pessimistic, and most likely scenarios) (Project Management Institute, 2021).

For instructional design projects, time estimation should account for the complexity of content, the experience level of team members, the number of review cycles required, and the availability of subject matter experts and stakeholders. Research suggests that inexperienced estimators typically underestimate task duration by 20-30%, making it important to build appropriate buffers into project timelines (Kerzner, 2017).

A useful approach for instructional design projects is to establish time standards for common activities. For example, experienced designers might estimate one hour of development time for every hour of finished course content, but this ratio can vary significantly based on content complexity, interactivity requirements, and media production needs (Clark & Mayer, 2016).

Buffer Time and Risk Management

Realistic project timelines must include appropriate buffer time to accommodate unexpected challenges, stakeholder delays, and iterative refinements. Risk management and contingency planning are crucial components of managing project constraints (eLearning Industry, 2023). Buffer time should be strategically placed at key decision points and after major deliverables to allow for stakeholder review and feedback incorporation.

Common risks in instructional design projects include subject matter expert unavailability, technology challenges, content accuracy issues, and changing requirements. The timeline should include contingency plans for these risks and appropriate buffer time to address them without compromising the overall project schedule (Greer & Lin, 2021).

Research indicates that projects with 15-20% buffer time built into their schedules are significantly more likely to be completed on time compared to projects with minimal or no buffer time (Dick et al., 2015).
