Module 3.2: Managing Faculty Relationships

Understanding Faculty Perspectives

The Faculty Mindset

Faculty members bring unique perspectives, expertise, and concerns to collaborative projects. Understanding these perspectives is crucial for instructional designers seeking to build effective partnerships. Faculty often approach collaboration with concerns about maintaining academic freedom, preserving content integrity, and ensuring that their disciplinary expertise is respected throughout the design process.

Common Faculty Concerns

Research has identified several recurring themes in faculty concerns about working with instructional designers:

Content Ownership:

Faculty may worry about losing control over their course content or having their expertise questioned.

Technology Adoption:

Some faculty feel uncomfortable with new learning technologies and may resist changes to familiar teaching methods.

Time Constraints:

Faculty often have competing demands on their time, including research, teaching, and service obligations. Therefore, they have limited time to dedicate to collaborative projects, which can lead to frustration if expectations are not aligned.

Role Clarity:

Misunderstandings about the roles and contributions of instructional designers, and how they complement rather than replaces faculty expertise, can create barriers to effective collaboration.

Building Empathy and Understanding

Instructional designers can build an effective working relationship with faculty by getting to know them beyond the role of instructor and content expert in a particular department (Academic Briefing, 2024). This involves understanding their research interests, teaching philosophy, and career goals. By demonstrating genuine interest in faculty members as whole individuals, instructional designers can create stronger foundations for collaboration.
