Module 3.6: Managing Faculty Relationships

Navigating Challenges and Conflicts

Common Sources of Conflict

While faculty and instructional designer work relationships are often amiable, tension and conflicts do arise, especially with instructors who have taught for many years and may resist changes to familiar teaching methods (Inside Higher Ed, 2017). No two faculty or projects are the same. However, there are some typicaly sources of conflict, such as philospohical differences. Disagreements about pedagogical approaches or learning theories can occur between faculty and designers, especially when it comes to assessment strategies. Resource constraints, such as limited time, budget, or technical resources are other common sources of conflict. If faculty are new to working with instrutional designers or experience designers, misunderstandings about roles, expectations, or project scope can occur. If they are new to teaching online or used to teaching a specific way, resistance to change can occur, with adopting new technologies or teaching methods being typical points of friction.

Conflict Resolution Strategies

Preventive Measures

Effective instructional design projects hinge on clear communication from the outseet. Establishing realistic expectations about communication protocols, project scope, and timelines help clarify formal understandings of stakeholders' roles and responsibilities. To maintain these relationships and communication channels, designers should build in regular check in points for feedback and adjustment as needed.

Active Resolution Techniques

Sometimes, conflicts arise despite best efforts. In these cases, active resolution techniques can help restore collaboration. First, address issues promptly to prevent escalation. Use active listening to understand the other party's perspective, and validate their concerns. Focus on your shared goals and student outomces and experiences. Then, work collaboratively, using objective criteria for decision-making, to find a solution that respects both parties' needs. If necessary, involve other stakeholders who can provide a more neutral perspective, such as an instructional design manager and/or a department chair to mediate the discussion and help reach a resolution.

Managing Difficult Relationships

Despite our best efforts, some faculty relationships (and collegial team relationships for that matter) may be particularly challenging. Remember to always maintain professionalism regardless of others' behavior. Sometimes folks have bad days and we don't know why, so it is often beneficial to provide others grace. However, should the behavior become threatening, abusive, or anything other than professional, maintain detailed records of interactions and decisions, including their rationale. Do not hesitate to seek support from your manager at any point in the process, even if it is just brainstorming how to respond. Ultimately, designers should maintain their personal well-bring and professional perspective when managing difficult relationships within a project.
